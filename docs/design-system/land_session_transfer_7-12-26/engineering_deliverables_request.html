What to ask the civil engineer for

Plan sets printed to paper-style files make Landscape work for its dinner. The same information exported in the engineer's native formats arrives as ready-to-use data — shapes, attributes, and quantities included. Every item below takes the engineering team minutes to produce; the ask belongs in the deliverables list of the next work order.

Best single ask

1. LandXML export

The civil industry's open exchange format. One file can carry the parcels (boundaries with names and areas), road alignments, ground surfaces, and complete pipe networks — every water, sewer, and storm run with its size, material, length, and depths. It is, quite literally, the quantity table and the site geometry as structured data.

What Landscape does with it: parcels become live map objects automatically; pipe networks become quantity lines in the phase budgets with zero manual takeoff.

Geometry

2. CAD drawing files (DWG or DXF)

The working drawings themselves rather than prints of them. Layered linework — parcel boundaries, rights-of-way, tracts, utilities — that can be read shape by shape. DXF is the open flavor; any CAD package exports it.

What Landscape does with it: exact parcel and tract shapes for the clickable site plan — no approximation, no tracing.

Geometry, mapping-ready

3. GIS parcel export (shapefile, GeoJSON, or KMZ)

Parcel and tract polygons with a data table attached — each shape carrying its ID, area, and land use — positioned in real-world coordinates. Most civil shops can export this from the same model that produced the plat.

What Landscape does with it: shapes drop straight onto the live map, aligned with aerials, county parcels, and flood layers — the full GIS-driven navigation, georeferenced.

Quantities

4. Quantity and earthwork reports (spreadsheet, not PDF)

Engineers already generate tabulated quantities — pipe schedules, pavement areas, cut/fill balances, plant schedules — for their own bidding process. Request them as spreadsheets rather than printed into the plan sheets.

What Landscape does with it: quantity lines map to your cost codes and become the engineering-based project budget, phase by phase.

Fallback

5. Vector PDFs, not scans

When only PDFs are on offer, insist on files plotted directly from CAD — they keep the underlying linework and searchable text. A scanned or flattened plan set is a photograph; a plotted one is still data wearing a PDF costume.

What Landscape does with it: readable quantity tables and recoverable geometry, versus the OCR slog.

Copy-paste for the next engineering work order

  1. LandXML export of parcels, alignments, surfaces, and all pipe networks — at each submittal milestone.
  2. DWG/DXF of the plat and improvement plans, current revision.
  3. GeoJSON or shapefile of parcel and tract polygons, in real-world coordinates.
  4. All quantity, earthwork, and planting schedules as Excel/CSV, matching the stamped set.
  5. All PDF plan sets plotted from CAD (vector), never scanned.
None of these change the engineer's scope or liability — they're exports of work already done. Some shops charge a nominal fee for CAD releases and may ask for a standard file-transfer disclaimer; both are worth it.
