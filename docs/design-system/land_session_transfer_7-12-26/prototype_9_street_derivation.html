Prototype 9

Red Valley Ranch Phase 1 — Street Specs Derived from the Plan Set

No CAD files needed for a first-pass quantity table. The plat and paving plans are drawn to a stated scale, the entitlement record states key dimensions outright, and city standard sections supply the widths. Cross-referencing the three produces street-by-street specs — every value tagged with how it was obtained. Quantities below are illustrative of the method; the real run happens when the document pipeline processes the full drawing set.

How each number is obtained — the cross-document method

1 · Scale from the sheet

Every plan sheet declares its scale in the title block ("1" = 200'" on the paving key map). Linework distance × scale = real-world feet. This is arithmetic, not guesswork.

2 · Stated dimensions

The record often just says it: the final plat staff report requires "a ½-mile, 2-lane paved road" on Farrell — that's 2,640 feet, stated in black and white.

3 · Widths from sections

Pavement and right-of-way widths come from the typical street sections in the plan set or the city's standard details — collector vs. local, each has a defined section.

4 · Cross-check

The plat, the paving plan, and the narrative all show the same streets. Where two documents agree, confidence rises; where they differ, a decision tile fires.

The verification loop closes this. These derived quantities are a first-pass estimate carrying "measured/assumed" tags on purpose. The moment the engineer's own quantity tabulations or a contractor bid schedule lands in the project, Landscape reconciles line by line: matches boost confidence to verified, deltas beyond tolerance fire decision tiles. Derived numbers start the budget months early; the engineer's numbers finish it. Both facts stay on the record.
